Every visitor to Sri Lanka faces this decision, usually while booking, usually
with insufficient information. The advice online tends to be strongly opinionated
in one direction or the other, and both camps are describing real experiences
that happen not to be yours.

Here is the comparison without the evangelism.

## The case for public transport

**It is extraordinarily cheap.** Colombo to Galle by expressway bus is around
LKR 390. Colombo to Kandy by train is USD 1–3. A tuk-tuk across town is a couple
of hundred rupees. A two-week trip covered entirely by public transport might
cost you fifty dollars in fares.

**It goes almost everywhere.** The bus network reaches places no rental company
will let you take a car.

**It removes an entire category of problems.** No parking. No navigation. No
responsibility for a vehicle you don't own in traffic you don't understand. No
police stops. No paperwork.

**You meet people.** This is not a small thing. Conversations on Sri Lankan
buses and trains are one of the more reliable pleasures of travelling here.

## The case against public transport

**It is slow, and slower than you think.** Buses stop constantly. Connections
don't align. A journey a car does in three hours can take five with a change in
the middle.

**Luggage is genuinely difficult.** Large suitcases on a crowded bus are
miserable for you and antisocial to everyone else.

**Rural coverage thins out.** Main corridors are excellent. The village fourteen
kilometres off the main road might see three buses a day.

**It's hard with small children**, in heat, with a schedule.

## The case for renting

**Freedom of route.** You can stop where you like, stay longer where it's good,
and reach places public transport doesn't serve.

**Efficiency for multi-stop trips.** If your itinerary has six stops in twelve
days, a vehicle saves you many hours of waiting.

**It's economical for groups.** Split four ways, a rental car often costs less
per person than four sets of tickets plus tuk-tuks at both ends.

**Luggage stops being a problem.**

## The case against renting

This is where people underestimate the difficulty, so it's worth being direct.

**Sri Lankan traffic is demanding.** Roads are narrow, lane discipline is
loose, and you'll share the carriageway with buses that consider themselves to
have right of way (they effectively do), tuk-tuks that stop without warning,
motorcycles overtaking on both sides, pedestrians, cyclists, dogs and
occasionally cattle. Overtaking on blind corners is normal. If you learned to
drive in northern Europe, this is a genuinely different activity.

**Average speeds are low.** Outside the expressways you should assume 30–40 km/h
on main roads and less in the hills. The map lies.

**There is paperwork, and it changed recently.** You cannot simply turn up with
a home licence. More on this below.

**Night driving is best avoided.** Unlit roads, unlit pedestrians, unlit
vehicles.

## The paperwork, which is the part people miss

To drive legally in Sri Lanka as a visitor you need a **Recognition Permit** or
a temporary local licence. Your home licence alone is not sufficient, and
neither is an International Driving Permit by itself.

Two routes as of 2026:

**At the airport.** Since August 2025 the Department of Motor Traffic operates a
24-hour counter in the arrivals hall at Bandaranaike International. It covers
cars, scooters and motorbikes — **not tuk-tuks**. Validity up to five months,
with fees starting around **LKR 15,000** for one month.

**Through the Automobile Association of Ceylon.** Since March 2025 the AAC
issues Recognition Permits only to holders of a valid **International Driving
Permit**, so you need to obtain the IDP in your home country first. Cost roughly
**LKR 7,000–8,000**, processing typically two to three hours.

If your licence isn't in English you'll need a certified translation.

**Tuk-tuks are a separate case.** Driving one as a foreigner legally requires a
specific temporary permit from the DMT, including a written test on road rules
and practical training. The many rental outfits offering tuk-tuks to tourists
frequently gloss over this.

Verify current requirements before you travel — these rules changed materially
in 2025 and may change again.

## The option most people forget

There is a third choice that the rent-versus-bus framing obscures: **hire a car
with a driver.**

You get the route freedom of a rental without doing the driving, the paperwork,
or the parking. You get local knowledge thrown in. Day rates typically include
vehicle and fuel, and for two or more people the cost gap versus self-drive is
smaller than most visitors assume once you account for the permit fees, fuel and
the insurance excess.

For the cultural triangle and hill country in particular — winding roads, poor
signage, long days — this is what most experienced visitors end up choosing.

## How to actually decide

Some rough guidance.

**Choose public transport if:** you're travelling solo or as a couple, you're
travelling light, your route follows main corridors, you have time, and the
journey is part of the point.

**Choose self-drive if:** you're confident in chaotic traffic, you're staying in
one region and exploring around it, you want maximum spontaneity, and you're
willing to handle the permit.

**Choose a driver if:** you have a multi-stop itinerary, you're travelling as a
family or group, your time is limited, or you simply don't want to spend your
holiday concentrating hard on not being hit by a bus.

**Or mix them.** A driver for the long inter-city legs, the train for Kandy to
Ella, buses on the expressway corridors, tuk-tuks locally. This is what a
well-designed Sri Lanka trip usually looks like, and it costs considerably less
than a driver for the full duration.

## The summary

Public transport is cheap, thorough and slow. Self-drive is liberating and
harder than it looks. A driver is the expensive, easy middle that most people
converge on for good reasons.

None of them is wrong. The mistake is deciding before you've looked at your
actual route.
