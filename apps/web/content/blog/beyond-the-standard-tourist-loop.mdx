There is a route through Sri Lanka that perhaps eight in ten first-time visitors
follow, in the same order, at the same pace. Land at Colombo airport. A night in
Negombo. North to Sigiriya. Down to Kandy. The train to Ella. Onward to the
south coast. Back along the expressway to Colombo and home.

It's a good route. We recommend a version of it ourselves for people with seven
days and no prior knowledge of the country. It respects the driving distances,
it hits genuinely extraordinary places, and it flows in a sensible direction.

But it has become so standard that it now produces a strange outcome: a great
many people visit Sri Lanka and come home having seen an almost identical
country to everyone else who went. Same rock, same temple, same train
photograph, same beach.

If you have more than a week, or you've been before, here is what sits just off
that line.

## The cultural triangle has more than one rock

Sigiriya is genuinely world-class and you should climb it. But the surrounding
region is dense with sites that receive a fraction of the visitors.

**Pidurangala** is the rock directly opposite Sigiriya. It costs a fraction of
the entry fee, takes about forty minutes to climb, and gives you the view that
every photograph of Sigiriya is actually taken from. Many people now do
Pidurangala at dawn and Sigiriya later, which is a reasonable compromise.

**Anuradhapura** is the older capital, and much larger — a sprawl of dagobas,
monastic ruins and sacred trees across an area you need a bicycle or a driver to
cover properly. It is far quieter than Sigiriya and, for anyone with an interest
in history, considerably more substantial.

**Polonnaruwa** is the second capital, more compact than Anuradhapura and
easier to see in half a day. The Gal Vihara Buddha figures carved into granite
are among the finest things in the country.

**Ritigala** is a forest monastery on a mountainside, largely unrestored, almost
always empty. It is the one that people remember.

## The hill country beyond Ella

Ella has become extremely busy. It is beautiful, and it has also acquired the
particular texture of a small town reorganised entirely around visitors.

**Haputale** sits twenty minutes away and has arguably the better view — Lipton's
Seat looks out over tea country to the southern plains, and on a clear morning
you can see the coast. **Ella Rock** and **Little Adam's Peak** are both worth
doing, but so is the walk from Haputale through the estates to Dambatenne.

**Belihuloya** and the **Knuckles Range** are where you go if you actually want
to walk. The Knuckles is a conservation area of cloud forest and village
footpaths, with genuine multi-day trekking and homestays. Almost no one on the
standard loop goes near it.

**Riverston** and the road across to Matale is one of the most spectacular
drives on the island and carries a fraction of the traffic of the Kandy–Nuwara
Eliya road.

## The coasts nobody drives to

The south coast between Galle and Tangalle is lovely and thoroughly discovered.
Two other coastlines are not.

**The east**, from Trincomalee down through Passekudah, Batticaloa and Arugam
Bay, is a genuinely different country — culturally Tamil and Muslim, quieter,
with wide empty beaches. Its season runs May to September, exactly opposite to
the south, which is precisely why so few people on the standard loop ever see
it: they come in the southern season and never cross over.

**The north**, around Jaffna and the islands beyond it, is the least-visited
part of the country by a wide margin. It has its own food, its own temples, its
own history, and it is a very long drive from anywhere on the standard route.
For a second or third visit it is the most rewarding direction you can go.

## Why the loop persists

It isn't laziness. The standard route exists for three sound reasons.

**Driving times.** Sri Lanka's roads are slow. The loop is efficient in a way
that alternatives often aren't. Going to the east or north costs you a full day
each way, and on a seven-day trip that's a large fraction of your time.

**Seasonality.** The loop works in the southern season, which is when most
European visitors come. Sending people east in January would be actively bad
advice.

**Infrastructure.** Hotels, guides and drivers are concentrated along it.

So the honest position is this: if you have seven days and it's your first
visit, do a version of the standard loop. It's standard because it works.

## What to do instead, by trip length

**Seven days, first visit.** Do the loop — but swap one stop. Trade a second
night in Ella for a night in Haputale, or replace Kandy with Anuradhapura if
you're more interested in ruins than in cities.

**Ten to fourteen days.** Add the Knuckles or Anuradhapura properly, and give
yourself two nights somewhere rural with nothing scheduled. This is where most
of the difference sits.

**Three weeks or more.** Go east or north. Commit to the drive. You'll see a
part of the country most visitors don't know exists.

**Second visit.** Skip the loop entirely. Jaffna, the east coast, the Knuckles,
and the tea country beyond Ella will fill three weeks without touching anything
you did the first time.

## The point isn't to avoid the famous places

Sigiriya deserves its reputation. The Kandy to Ella train is genuinely one of
the great rail journeys. Galle Fort at golden hour is worth the crowds.

The point is that these are the beginning of the country rather than a summary
of it. The loop shows you Sri Lanka's greatest hits. What it doesn't show you is
the place itself — which mostly happens in the towns you drive through without
stopping, in the hours between the sites, in the parts of the island nobody has
organised around your visit yet.

Build your route from the famous things. Then take one deliberate detour off it.
That detour will almost certainly be the part you talk about when you get home.
