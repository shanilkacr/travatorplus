Ask someone who visited Sri Lanka in 2012 and again last year and you'll get a
complicated answer. The country is more comfortable to travel in than it was.
It's also, in specific places, considerably busier.

This is worth thinking about honestly rather than either dismissing it or
performing anxiety about it.

## What actually changed

Sri Lanka's tourism has had a violent decade. Steady growth after the civil war
ended in 2009. The 2019 Easter bombings collapsed arrivals overnight. The
pandemic closed things entirely. The 2022 economic crisis brought fuel queues
and power cuts. Then a genuine recovery.

Each collapse was followed by a rebound, and each rebound concentrated in the
same handful of places — because those are the places with the infrastructure,
the marketing and the Instagram presence.

The result isn't a country that's uniformly crowded. It's a country where about
eight locations carry a wildly disproportionate share of visitors, and the other
ninety-five percent of the island sees very few.

## Where you'll actually feel it

**Ella** has changed most. A hill town of a few thousand people now has a main
street of cafés, bars and tour agencies. Nine Arch Bridge at train time is a
scrum. It is still beautiful; it is no longer quiet.

**Sigiriya** at peak hours means queuing on the staircases. The rock has a
finite number of steps and they bottleneck.

**Mirissa** whale watching has had genuine welfare concerns, with boats crowding
animals. Choose an operator that keeps distance and doesn't chase.

**Galle Fort** is small, walled and popular. Cruise days are noticeably busy.

**The Kandy–Ella train** sells out in peak season, and the famous door-hanging
photograph now involves a queue.

Meanwhile: **Anuradhapura**, **the east coast**, **the Knuckles**, **Jaffna**
and virtually anywhere rural remain quiet.

## What over-tourism actually costs a place

It's worth being specific, because the abstract version invites eye-rolling.

**Water.** Hotels with pools in areas where villages are on restricted supply.

**Waste.** Small towns absorbing volumes of plastic and sewage their
infrastructure was never sized for.

**Property.** Guesthouse economics pricing locals out of their own towns —
visible in Ella and parts of the south coast.

**Wildlife.** Jeep crowding at Yala around leopard sightings, and boat crowding
off Mirissa.

**Culture becoming performance.** The point at which a ritual is scheduled for
visitors rather than attended by residents.

None of this means don't come. Tourism is one of Sri Lanka's largest sources of
foreign exchange and employs a very large number of people, many in places with
few alternatives. The 2020–2022 collapse was devastating for those households.
The answer isn't fewer visitors. It's better-distributed ones.

## The practical things that help

**Go where others don't.** This is the single highest-impact choice. Anuradhapura
instead of a fourth day in the triangle. Haputale instead of a third night in
Ella. The east coast in its season. Your money goes further in places that need
it more, and you'll have a better time.

**Travel in shoulder season.** April and October spread demand and cost less.

**Stay in locally-owned places.** Homestays, family guesthouses and small hotels
keep revenue in the district. International chains repatriate most of it.

**Hire local guides.** At Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, the Knuckles — a local
guide costs little, dramatically improves what you understand, and puts money
directly into the local economy.

**Eat locally.** The unmarked lunch place is cheaper, better, and its money stays
put.

**Choose wildlife operators carefully.** Ask about distance policies before
booking. At Yala, going early and accepting you might not see a leopard is
better than joining a convoy that surrounds one. Avoid anywhere offering
elephant riding or captive-animal photo opportunities.

**Refill rather than buy.** Bottled water is the largest single waste stream
tourists generate here. A filter bottle handles it.

**Don't haggle to the floor.** Negotiating is normal. Grinding a tuk-tuk driver
down over the equivalent of forty pence is not a win.

## The cultural things that matter

These aren't about crowding — they're about not being the visitor everyone
remembers for the wrong reason.

**Buddha images are not a backdrop.** Turning your back on one for a photograph
is a genuine offence and has resulted in visitors being detained and deported.
Tattoos of the Buddha have caused people to be refused entry at the airport.
Stand beside, not in front.

**Cover shoulders and knees at religious sites**, and remove shoes and hats.
This applies to Buddhist temples, Hindu kovils and mosques alike.

**Dress more conservatively away from the beach.** Beachwear is for the beach.
In towns, in the hills, and especially in the east and north, cover up.

**Ask before photographing people.** Particularly women, particularly at
worship.

**Public displays of affection** are read differently here than in Europe.

**The head is sacred and feet are unclean.** Don't pat children on the head;
don't point your soles at people or at religious images.

## The honest conclusion

Sri Lanka is not "ruined". It is a country where a small number of famous places
are working near capacity while most of the island remains genuinely uncrowded
and — in many districts — economically dependent on visitors who never arrive.

If you go where everyone goes, at the time everyone goes, and stay in
internationally-owned hotels, you'll contribute to the strain and see the
version of the country that's been arranged for you.

If you shift a couple of nights sideways, travel in a shoulder month, and spend
your money with people who live there, you'll have a better trip and leave the
place slightly better than a more conventional itinerary would.

That's not sacrifice. In our experience it's just the better holiday.
