There is no single best way to travel around Sri Lanka. There are five, and the
right one changes depending on where you are, how far you're going, and what
time it is.

Here's an honest comparison.

## Ride-hailing apps: PickMe and Uber

**PickMe** is the local app and the one to install first. It covers considerably
more of the island than Uber, and — importantly — it lets you order **tuk-tuks
as well as cars**, which is often what you actually want.

**Uber** operates in Colombo and, patchily, in Galle and Kandy. Outside those
areas coverage thins out quickly.

The practical difference beyond coverage: Uber quotes a fixed price when you
confirm. PickMe works more like a metered taxi, so the final fare can differ
from the estimate if the route changes. Neither is dishonest; they're just
different models.

Both start from around **LKR 180** for short trips. App-based rides typically
come in **10–17% cheaper** than negotiating the same journey with a roadside
tuk-tuk, and — the real benefit — you skip the negotiation entirely.

An airport-to-Colombo tuk-tuk via the app runs roughly LKR 2,000; a car around
LKR 3,000.

**Use apps when:** you're in Colombo or a major town, you want a fixed
expectation of price, or you don't want to haggle. **Don't rely on them when:**
you're rural, or it's late at night outside a city.

## Tuk-tuks, hailed on the street

Still the backbone of short-distance travel everywhere, and essential where the
apps don't reach.

The rule is simple: **agree the fare before you get in.** Many tuk-tuks now have
meters, and asking for the meter is entirely normal — *"meter dhanna"*. Where
there's no meter, a reasonable rate is roughly LKR 80–120 per kilometre, more in
tourist areas.

If you're being quoted something that feels absurd, it probably is. Walking
fifty metres and asking a second driver is a remarkably effective negotiating
tactic.

Tuk-tuks are open-sided, which is wonderful in good weather and character-building
in rain. They're slower than cars over distance and genuinely uncomfortable for
anything beyond about an hour.

**Use for:** anything under about 15km, or where no other option exists.

## Buses

Sri Lanka's bus network is extensive, frequent, and extremely cheap. It goes
essentially everywhere.

Two tiers matter. **Standard buses** (usually red CTB or private white ones) are
very cheap, very frequent, and often very full. **Intercity air-conditioned
coaches**, including the expressway services, are more comfortable and still
inexpensive — Colombo to Galle sits around LKR 390, Colombo to Matara around
LKR 393 on standard inter-provincial rates.

Buses don't really do timetables in the way Europeans expect; on busy routes
they simply run continuously. On rural routes they may go a few times a day, and
that timetable matters a great deal.

**Use for:** intercity travel on main corridors, especially the expressways, and
anywhere you want to travel cheaply and don't mind the journey being part of the
experience. **Avoid when:** you have a lot of luggage, tight timing, or motion
sensitivity — Sri Lankan bus driving is committed.

## Trains

The train is the best way to travel in Sri Lanka when the train happens to go
where you're going. That's the catch: the network is limited, and it's slow.

The **Kandy to Ella** hill-country line is routinely listed among the world's
great rail journeys and deserves it — six or seven hours through tea country,
tunnels and viaducts, with the doors open. The **coastal line** south from
Colombo to Galle and Matara runs beside the sea for much of its length.

**Colombo to Kandy costs roughly USD 1–3** depending on class. Reserved seats in
first and second class open for booking **30 days in advance** and the popular
tourist services sell out — particularly the Kandy–Ella morning trains in peak
season.

Third class is unreserved, cheap, crowded, and where most of the fun is. Second
class unreserved is a reasonable compromise.

**Use for:** Kandy–Ella, and the coastal run. **Don't use for:** getting
anywhere quickly, or anywhere the line doesn't go — which is most of the north,
east and interior.

## Private driver

A car with a driver for the duration of your trip is what a large proportion of
visitors end up doing, and it isn't the lazy option people assume.

Sri Lankan roads are slow, winding and busy. A good driver-guide knows the
routes, handles the parking and the touts, tells you what you're looking at, and
becomes genuinely useful when something goes wrong. Day rates typically include
the vehicle and fuel.

The trade is cost and a degree of separation from the country. You'll see a lot
more and meet a lot fewer people.

**Use for:** multi-stop itineraries, families, tight schedules, the hill country
and the cultural triangle, or anywhere you'd otherwise spend half your day
working out connections.

## What most people should actually do

The best trips usually mix these rather than committing to one.

A sensible default for a first visit: **private driver for the long
inter-city legs** through the cultural triangle and hill country, **the train
for Kandy to Ella** because it's an experience rather than transport,
**expressway buses** for the straightforward runs like Colombo to Galle, and
**PickMe or a street tuk-tuk** for everything local once you're based
somewhere.

That combination costs meaningfully less than a driver for the whole trip and
gives you a far more varied experience than any single mode.

## Practical notes

**Get a local SIM at the airport.** Dialog and Mobitel both have counters in
arrivals; a tourist data package is inexpensive. Every app above depends on it.

**Carry small notes.** Bus conductors and tuk-tuk drivers rarely have change for
large denominations.

**Download offline maps.** Coverage is good on main roads and unreliable in the
hills and the interior.

**Build in more time than the distance suggests.** Sri Lankan roads average far
lower speeds than the map implies. A hundred kilometres can easily be three
hours.
