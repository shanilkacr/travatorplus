Renting your own vehicle in Sri Lanka gives you a kind of freedom no other
arrangement matches. It also carries more administrative and practical
complexity than most visitors expect, and the requirements were substantially
revised during 2025.

This is what you actually need to know before you book anything.

## The paperwork comes first

You cannot legally drive here on your home licence alone. You need a **Sri
Lankan Recognition Permit** or a temporary local driving licence, and you should
sort this before or immediately on arrival — not when a rental agent asks for
it.

### Route one: the airport counter

Since **August 2025**, the Department of Motor Traffic has operated a **24-hour
counter in the arrivals hall at Bandaranaike International Airport**, near the
SPAR supermarket. This is the most convenient option and did not exist before.

- Covers **cars, scooters and motorbikes**
- Does **not** cover tuk-tuks or heavy vehicles
- Valid for up to **five months**
- Fees start around **LKR 15,000** for one month

### Route two: the Automobile Association of Ceylon

Since **30 March 2025**, the AAC issues Recognition Permits **only to holders of
a valid International Driving Permit**. You must obtain the IDP in your home
country before you travel — it cannot be issued here.

- Cost roughly **LKR 7,000–8,000**
- Processing typically **two to three hours**
- Validity generally matches your IDP

An IDP on its own is not a licence. It's a multilingual translation of your home
licence, and Sri Lanka requires both documents together.

### If your licence isn't in English

You'll need a certified English translation from your embassy or an authorised
body. This catches out visitors from Japan, China, and much of the Middle East
and Eastern Europe every week.

### Tuk-tuks are a special case

Despite how freely they're rented to tourists, driving a tuk-tuk as a foreigner
legally requires a **specific temporary permit from the DMT**, which involves a
written examination on Sri Lankan road rules and practical training with a
certified instructor. The DMT clarified this in May 2025.

Many rental operators will hand you keys without mentioning any of it. That
becomes your problem at a police checkpoint, and more seriously, it may void
your insurance.

## Choosing the vehicle

**Scooter (100–125cc).** Cheapest and most fun. Genuinely dangerous in Sri
Lankan traffic if you're inexperienced. Fine for pottering around a coastal town;
poor for long distances or the hills.

**Motorbike (150cc+).** Better for distance and hills. Requires real competence.

**Car.** The sensible default for families and anyone travelling in the wet
season. Small hatchbacks handle narrow roads better than anything large.

**4x4.** Only necessary if you're going seriously off-road. The main road network
does not require it.

**Tuk-tuk.** Romantic, slow, exposed, and legally complicated as above.

## The inspection, before you sign anything

Do this every time, without exception, and photograph everything.

- **Walk the vehicle and film it.** Every scratch, dent and scuff, with the agent
  present. This is your only defence against a damage claim at return.
- **Check the tyres**, including tread depth and the spare.
- **Test the brakes** before leaving the forecourt.
- **Check the lights** — headlights, indicators, brake lights. Frequently faulty.
- **Check the horn.** In Sri Lanka this is safety equipment, not an insult.
- **Confirm the fuel policy** and photograph the gauge at pickup.
- **Look at the insurance paperwork.** Ask specifically what the excess is and
  what is excluded. Many cheap rentals carry very high excesses or exclude
  single-vehicle accidents entirely.
- **Confirm the vehicle is legally registered and taxed**, and that the revenue
  licence is current.
- **Get the agent's mobile number** and check it works before you drive away.

## On the road

**Buses have de facto right of way.** Not legally, but practically. Yield.

**Horns mean "I am here"**, not "you idiot". Use yours on blind corners.

**Overtaking happens on blind bends** constantly. Assume oncoming traffic on
your side of the road at all times in the hills.

**Speeds are low.** Assume 30–40 km/h averages off the expressways.

**Avoid driving at night.** Unlit pedestrians, cyclists and vehicles are the
single biggest hazard.

**Expressways require a toll** and have minimum speeds. Motorcycles and tuk-tuks
are prohibited on them.

**Police checkpoints are routine.** Have your permit, passport, and rental
agreement accessible. Be polite; it's almost always brief.

**Parking in towns** is informal and often attended by someone who will expect a
small tip.

## The costs people forget

Beyond the daily rate: the permit (LKR 7,000–15,000), fuel, expressway tolls,
the insurance excess if anything happens, parking tips, and — the big one — the
deposit, which can be substantial and is sometimes slow to return.

Add these up before comparing against a driver. The gap narrows considerably.

## When not to self-drive

Be honest with yourself. If you haven't driven in South Asian traffic before, if
you're arriving jet-lagged, if you're doing a long multi-stop route, if it's the
height of the monsoon, or if you're going into the hill country without
experience of mountain roads — the risk-to-reward is poor.

There is no shame in the alternative, and most experienced visitors to Sri Lanka
end up there.
